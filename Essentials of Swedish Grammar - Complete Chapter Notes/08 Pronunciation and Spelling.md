Pronunciation and spelling
Pronunciation and spelling have already been described briefly in 1.1.
Vowels and consonants
The sounds of all languages are divided into two major groups: vowels
(vokaler) and consonants (konsonanter). The vowels can be sub-divided into
vowels and diphthongs. A diphthong (diftong) is a combination of two vowel
sounds. In standard British English there are 12 vowels, 9 diphthongs and 24
consonants.
Many of these sounds do not have their own letters. Instead they are
represented by combinations of letters, like th, ch, oy and other special
spelling patterns.
The Swedish language has 9 vowels and 18 consonants; it does not have
any diphthongs, except in certain dialects.
Vowels Consonants
i e ä y ö o å a u p t k b d g s s j t j h
f v j 1 r m n ng
Several of the consonant sounds in Swedish do not have a letter of their own.
They are spelt by using a combination of several letters which is read as one
sound. This will be explained later on.
In Swedish, as in English, you can often hear from a person’s pronunci­
ation where he or she comes from. Pronunciation varies from one part of the
country to another. In the following we shall mainly describe the pronunci­
ation in Stockholm and central Sweden.
How sounds in language are made.
Voiced and voiceless sounds
To understand the difference between vowels and consonants you need to
know something about the ways in which the sounds in a language are made.
These sounds are formed when air passes from the lungs through the larynx,
the throat and the mouth. In the larynx the air passes through a narrow
passage. The edges of this passage form the vocal chords. If the vocal chords
are brought towards each other, they are caused to vibrate by the air from
the lungs. This creates a vocal tone. You can test this out on yourself by
saying a longdrawn aaaaa. If you put two fingers on your larynx, you will be
able to feel the vibrations. Sounds made with a vocal tone are called voiced
sounds (tonande ljud). The opposite of voiced is voiceless (tonlös), that is

the consonant p, for example in the word paper. Notice how the lips close
completely for an instant when you say the p ’s and open when you say the

Length and stress
In 1.1 we pointed out that Swedish distinguishes between long and short
vowels in pronunciation. English does not have the same clear distinction,
though the words hit and heat can be said to have a short and a long i sound
respectively.
The spelling of short and long vowels in Swedish is not very consistent. A
single vowel (letter) is used in writing both when the vowel is pronounced
long and when it is pronounced short. To make it easier for you to pro­
nounce the Swedish words we will use a special marking system. A dash (_) is
placed under the long vowels and a dot (.) under the short ones. These
marks are not used in ordinary written texts in Swedish:
Normal text Marked text
tal speech tal
tall pine tall
rik rich rik
risk risk rjsk
The words in the examples above have only one vowel. But many words
have more than one vowel, which creates another difficulty in Swedish: stress
(betoning). Stress means that you say one part of a word with more emphasis
than the rest. In Swedish it is usually the first vowel that is stressed (betonad),
but as in English there are also quite a few words which do not follow this
rule. One example is the word banan ‘banana’. As in English, the first vowel
is not stressed but the second one is, though this is, of course, not indicated
by the spelling.
There is a connection between stress and length in Swedish:
A stressed vowel may be either long or short.
An unstressed vowel is always short.

8.4 Acute and grave accent
In Swedish pronunciation there is another feature which has no equivalent in
English. It is the difference between what are called the acute and the grave
accent (akut accent, grav accent). Certain words differ only in the accent they
have, for example:
Acute accent Grave accent
anden the duck anden the spirit
stegen the steps stegen the ladder
vaken the hole in the ice vaken awake
The stress is on the same vowel in these pairs of words, but they are
pronounced with different ‘tunes’, which mark the only difference between
them. This difference is quite difficult to hear if Swedish is not your native
language. It may help you to think of the acute accent as being the usual
tone, with a low falling tone on the second syllable. The grave accent has a
higher falling tone on the second syllable, rather like the way you say side in

misunderstood if you use the wrong accent. So you can wait until a more

The vowels ö and ä are pronounced in a special way when they come before
an r. This special sound may be marked with a small raised r, thus: ör, är.
You can hear a clear difference between these sounds and an ordinary ö or ä

Note that a short ä sound is pronounced in this special way even when it is
spelt with an e. (As mentioned in 8 . 6 the e sound and the short ä sound are

Pronunciation of the letter o
The letter o can cause problems in Swedish. The sound o is always spelt with
this letter. But the trouble is that the sound å is also spelt with the letter o in
certain words. In particular a short å sound is often spelt in this way. So
when you learn a word with the short å sound, you must make sure to learn
whether it is spelt with the letter å or the letter o. All the following words are
pronounced with a short å sound although the spelling varies:
å sound lopp race
mått measurement, size
jobb job
A short o sound is actually rather unusual in Swedish, so you may reasonably
suppose that the letter o will be pronounced as a short o sound. The words
which in fact have a short o sound are, however, spelt with the letter o, for
example ost ‘cheese’.
Because of these difficulties you should always pay special attention to the
pronunciation of a word which is spelt with the letter o. There are the

8.9 Swedish consonants
All consonants are formed by the air which passes through the windpipe and
emerges at the mouth being obstructed at some point (see 8.2). Consonants
differ from each other in three different ways:
• The obstacle to the passage of air can occur at different points.
• The passage of air may be obstructed in different ways.
• Some consonants are voiced and others are voiceless (8.2).
We shall look particularly closely here at two groups of consonants. They are
the stops and the fricatives. The latter group comprises several sounds that
are difficult to learn.
8.9.1 Swedish stops
W hen you say a stop, the stream of air is completely blocked for an instant.
That is what happens when you say a p in the word ape. You close your lips
when you say a p. (You can check this by looking in a m irror.) O ther stops
are t and k. They are different from p because the blockage occurs at another
point. W hen you say t, you raise the tip of your tongue to a point just behind
your top front teeth; when you say k the back of your tongue is raised and
touches your soft palate so that the stream of air is checked for an instant.
Swedish has the following stops:
Voiceless P t k
Voiced b d 9

Just as in English, the stops in the top and bottom row are paired off, as the
stream of air is blocked at the same point. The difference is that the upper
stop in each pair is voiceless, while the lower one is voiced (8.2). These
sounds are all pronounced almost identically in Swedish and English. One
slight difference is that t and d in Swedish are pronounced with the tongue
against the back of the upper front teeth, not as in English with the tongue
just above the front teeth.
8.9.2 Swedish fricatives
W hen you say a fricative, the stream of air passes through a narrow opening,
which makes a kind of hissing sound. But the opening is not so narrow as to
stem the air stream completely.
The Swedish fricatives are:
Voiceless f s Sj tj*
Voiced V
As you can see, Swedish does not have the voiced equivalents of s, sj and tj
as English has. Thus there is no z sound in Swedish as there is in English
words like busy, nose. Nor does Swedish have the th fricatives of English
words like thing and that.
f, v and s have identical pronunciations in English and Swedish, and so
cause no trouble.
The letter combination s 4 -j in Swedish is pronounced as one sound. The
same is true of the combination t + j .
The sound represented by sj is rather like the English sh sound in shop,
but is formed further back in the mouth with the lips more rounded. This
sound is also spelt sk, skj, stj, sch or ch (see 8.12-8.13).
The sound represented by tj is the voiceless counterpart of the Swedish /,
which is very similar to the y in the English word young (see 8.10). It can be
made by saying a long-drawn Swedish j, but suppressing the voicing to make
the sound voiceless (see 8.2). It is a little like the English ch sound in church
without the t sound at the beginning. It may also be spelt k, kj.
It is important in Swedish to keep the two sounds sj and tj separate. It is

8.15 Doubled consonants
As we have seen, the length of vowels is not given by the spelling in Swedish.
But for consonants it is. The basic rule is:
A doubled consonant is pronounced as a long consonant sound.
As well as there being a difference between long and short vowels, there is
also a difference between long and short consonants, as in the following
examples:
hat - hatt sil - sjll rys - ryss
hate hat strainer herring shudder Russian (noun)
Long consonants are not at all common in English and only occur between
two words, for example: ‘black coffee’, ‘good dog’; but they are very
common in Swedish.
One peculiarity of Swedish spelling is that the letter k is not doubled.
Instead ck is used:
lack varnish tacka thank
ck replaces kk.
The pronunciation of long and short consonants does not usually cause any
great difficulty. Also, the difference between a long and a short vowel is very
much more important than the difference between a long and a short
consonant. In Swedish a long vowel cannot come directly before a long
consonant; it can only come before a short consonant. So you can usually
work out from the spelling whether a vowel should be pronounced long or
short. There is one condition: that you know whether the vowel is stressed or
not. So the following rules apply only to words that contain one vowel
only, since if there is only one vowel it must be stressed (when the word is
pronounced in isolation. See 8.3):

### 8.1 Vowels And Consonants

#### Swedish Sounds

| Type | Sounds / Letters |
|---|---|
| Vowels | i, e, ä, y, ö, o, å, a, u |
| Consonants | p, t, k, b, d, g, s, sj, tj, h, f, v, j, l, r, m, n, ng |

#### Key Points

- Some Swedish consonant sounds do not have a single letter.
- Letter combinations may be read as one sound.
- Pronunciation varies regionally; the book mainly describes central Swedish/Stockholm pronunciation.

### 8.2 How Sounds In Language Are Made

#### Voiced And Voiceless

| Type | Explanation | Example Test |
|---|---|---|
| Voiced | Vocal cords vibrate. | Say a long `vvvvv`. |
| Voiceless | No vocal tone. | Say a long `sssss`. |

All vowels are voiced. Consonants may be voiced or voiceless.

#### Vowels Vs Consonants

| Sound Type | Airflow |
|---|---|
| Vowels | Air passes freely through the mouth. |
| Consonants | Air is constricted or blocked somewhere. |

### 8.3 Length And Stress

#### Length Marking

| Normal Text | Marked Text | English |
|---|---|---|
| tal | tal | speech |
| tall | tall | pine |
| rik | rik | rich |
| risk | risk | risk |

Only the stressed vowel is marked. An unmarked vowel is unstressed and short.

#### Stress Patterns

| Stress On First Vowel | English |
|---|---|
| vila | rest |
| villa | house |
| silar | strainers |
| sillar | herrings |

| Stress Not On First Vowel | English |
|---|---|
| magasin | warehouse |
| tablett | tablet |
| teater | theatre |
| paket | packet |
| metall | metal |
| betona | stress |
| försök | try |
| behålla | keep |

### 8.4 Acute And Grave Accent

#### Examples

| Acute Accent | Grave Accent |
|---|---|
| anden - the duck | anden - the spirit |
| stegen - the steps | stegen - the ladder |
| vaken - the hole in the ice | vaken - awake |

#### Practical Point

The accent difference is difficult for many learners to hear at first. The book notes that using the wrong accent rarely causes misunderstanding, so it can be postponed until a more advanced level.

### 8.5 Swedish Long Vowels

#### Long Vowels

| Vowel | Approximation | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| i | like `bee`, more closed | vin, vila |
| e | like French `é` | te, leka |
| ä | like `men`, but longer | läsa, häl |
| y | like French/German `u`, rounded | ny, lysa |
| ö | like French `eu` | öl, dö |
| o | like `oo` in school | ros, sol |
| å | like `ou` in ought, closer | år, låna |
| a | like father, further back | vara, ja |
| u | difficult; lips close together | hus, mur |

#### Important Contrasts

| Contrast | How To Practice |
|---|---|
| i / y | Say long `i`, then round the lips for `y`. |
| e / ö | Say long `e`, then round the lips for `ö`. |

Long `i`, `y`, `u`, and `o` may end with a consonant-like glide.

### 8.6 Swedish Short Vowels

#### Important Contrasts

| Contrast | Examples |
|---|---|
| long/short u | bus/buss, hus/hund, rusa/rusta, sluta/slutta |
| short e = short ä | sett/sätt |

#### Other Long/Short Pairs

| Long | Short |
|---|---|
| vin | vinn |
| syl | sylt |
| föl | föll |
| rot | rott |
| hål | håll |
| hat | hatt |

#### Unstressed Vowels

Short vowels keep distinct pronunciations even when unstressed; they are not reduced to a neutral `e`.

### 8.7 Pronunciation Of Ö And Ä Before R

#### Examples

| Ordinary Sound | Before r | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| ö | ör | hö / hör |
| ö | ör | dött / dörr |
| ä | är | häl / här |
| ä | är | mätt / märr |

Short `ä` before `r` can be spelt with `e`, as in `herre`.

#### Practical Point

Recognition is more important than perfect production at the beginner stage. Some Swedish regions pronounce these differently.

### 8.8 Pronunciation Of The Letter O

#### Main Patterns

| Letter | Sound | Example | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| o | o | sol | normal long `o` |
| o | å | lov | exception |
| o | short o | ost | relatively few words |
| o | short å | jobb | common short `å` spelling |

The letter `å` itself is always pronounced as an `å` sound.

#### Compare

| Word | Sound | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| hov | o | hoof |
| hov | å | court |
| håv | å | bag net |
| kol | å | coal |
| kål | å | cabbage |
| kort | o | card |
| kort | å | short |

### 8.9 Swedish Consonants

#### Three Dimensions

| Dimension | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Place | Where the airflow is obstructed. |
| Manner | How the airflow is obstructed. |
| Voicing | Whether the vocal cords vibrate. |

The book then focuses on two important groups: stops and fricatives.

### 8.9.1 Swedish Stops

#### Swedish Stops

| Voiceless | Voiced |
|---|---|
| p | b |
| t | d |
| k | g |

#### Notes

`t` and `d` in Swedish are pronounced with the tongue against the back of the upper front teeth, slightly different from English.

### 8.9.2 Swedish Fricatives

#### Swedish Fricatives

| Voiceless | Voiced |
|---|---|
| f | v |
| s |  |
| sj |  |
| tj |  |

Swedish lacks English-style `z` and `th` fricatives.

#### Special Sounds

| Sound | Notes | Spellings |
|---|---|---|
| sj | Similar to English `sh`, but farther back and more rounded. | sj, sk, skj, stj, sch, ch |
| tj | Voiceless counterpart of Swedish `j`; somewhat like English `ch` without initial `t`. | tj, k, kj |

### 8.10 Other Consonants

#### Consonants

| Sound | Example | Note |
|---|---|---|
| m | mat | close to English |
| n | natt | close to English |
| ng | säng, sång | pronounced like English `singer`, not `finger` |
| h | ha, hamn | close to English |
| j | ja, jo-jo | like English `y` in `yes` |
| l | lära | always a clear `l`, like English `love` |
| r | röd | usually slightly rolled; not dropped word-finally |

### 8.11 Consonant Combinations

#### Important Patterns

| Pattern | Example | Note |
|---|---|---|
| kn | knä, knacka | `k` is pronounced before `n` word-initially. |
| gn | gnaga | `g` is pronounced before `n` word-initially. |
| gn after vowel | vagn, lugn | pronounced as `ng + n`. |

| Sound | Spellings / Examples |

### 8.13 G K And Sk Before Front Vowels

#### Front And Back Vowels

| Front Vowels | Back Vowels |
|---|---|
| i, e, ö, ä, y | o, å, a, u |

#### Before Front Vowels

| Letter(s) | Sound | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| g | j | ge, göra |
| k | tj | kyla, köra |
| sk | sj | skina, skön |

Before back vowels, these letters are pronounced in the usual way.

#### After R And L

| Pattern | Sound | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| rg | rj | berg, torg |
| lg | lj | helg |

### 8.14 Rt Rd Rn And Rs

#### Listening Pairs

| Pair | English |
|---|---|
| fat / fart | saucer / speed |
| bod / bord | shed / table |
| ton / torn | tone / tower |
| mos / mors | pulp / mother's |

#### Across Word Boundaries

The same pronunciation may occur when a word ends in `r` and the next word begins with `t`, `d`, `n`, or `s`.

| Pattern | Example | English |
|---|---|---|
| rt | Han dricker te. | He is drinking tea. |
| rd | Förstår du? | Do you understand? |
| rn | Har ni tid? | Have you got time? |
| rs | Du kommer för sent. | You are too late. |

### 8.15 Doubled Consonants

#### Examples

| Short/Long Contrast | English |
|---|---|
| hat / hatt | hate / hat |
| sil / sill | strainer / herring |
| rys / ryss | shudder / Russian |

#### Spelling Rule

`k` is not doubled as `kk`; Swedish uses `ck`.

| Spelling | Example |
|---|---|
| ck | lack, tacka |

#### Vowel Length Rules

| Condition | Vowel |
|---|---|
| stressed and not followed by a consonant | long |
| stressed and followed by one consonant | long |
| followed by a doubled consonant | short |
| followed by two or more consonants | short |
| unstressed | short |

Adding an ending normally does not change length and stress.

### 8.16 Doubling Of M And N

#### M

The letter `m` is doubled only between two vowels. Otherwise, only one `m` is written even if it is long after a short stressed vowel.

| Between Two Vowels | End / Other Position |
|---|---|
| komma | Kom! |
| rymmet | ett rum |
| damen | en dam |

#### N

`n` is not doubled at the end of a few common words.

| Single n Word | Related Form |
|---|---|
| kan | kunna |
| en man | mannen |
| en |  |
| han, hon, den | personal pronouns |
| min, din, sin | possessives |

`n` is never doubled before `d` and `t`.

| Form | Related Verb |
|---|---|
| känt | känna |
| kände | känns |

### 8.17 Capital And Small Letters

#### Nationality Words

Nationality words begin with a small letter in Swedish.

| Swedish | English |
|---|---|
| engelska | English |
| tyska | German |
| franska | French |
| finsk | Finnish |
| svensk | Swedish |
| greker | Greeks |

#### Days, Months, Seasons

Names of days, months, and seasons begin with small letters.

| Swedish | English |
|---|---|
| fredag | Friday |
| vintern | winter |
| december | December |
| oktober | October |

#### Titles Of Works

In names of films, plays, and books, only the first word is capitalized.

| Swedish Title Style | English |
|---|---|
| Gudarna måste vara tokiga | The Gods Must Be Mad |

## 3. Summary

### 8 Pronunciation And Spelling

##### 中文总结

第 8 章是瑞典语发音和拼写的核心章节。重点包括元音长短、重音、`sj/tj` 等辅音、`g/k/sk` 在前元音前的读音、双写辅音与短元音的关系，以及瑞典语大小写规则。

##### 学习建议

- 学新词时同时标记 stress 和 vowel length。
- `sj`, `tj`, `sk`, `g/k before front vowels` 需要单独听辨。
- 拼写规则要和发音规则一起学，不要只看字母。

### 8.1 Vowels And Consonants

##### 中文总结

瑞典语有 9 个元音和 18 个辅音。某些辅音不是单个字母表示，而是由字母组合表示，例如后面会学到的 `sj`, `tj`, `ng`。

##### 检查点

- 是否能列出 9 个瑞典语元音？
- 是否知道 Swedish normally has no diphthongs？
- 是否知道字母组合可表示一个音？

### 8.2 How Sounds In Language Are Made

##### 中文总结

发音时气流从肺部经过喉部和口腔。声带振动形成 voiced sound；不振动是 voiceless sound。所有元音都是 voiced，辅音则有清浊区别。

##### 检查点

- 是否能用 `v` 和 `s` 感受清浊区别？
- 是否知道所有元音都是 voiced？
- 是否能解释 vowel 和 consonant 的气流差异？

### 8.3 Length And Stress

##### 中文总结

瑞典语元音有长短区别，重音通常落在一个元音上。长短标记只标在重读元音下；未标记元音通常为非重读短元音。

##### 检查点

- 是否知道 vowel length 和 stress 要一起看？
- 是否知道一个词通常只有一个重读元音？
- 是否能比较 `vila` 与 `villa`？

### 8.4 Acute And Grave Accent

##### 中文总结

acute/grave accent 是瑞典语音调差异，不是字母上的重音符号。初学时不必过度纠结，但要知道它存在。

##### 检查点

- 是否知道 acute 和 grave 是发音“调型”差异？
- 是否知道它们不等同于拼写符号？
- 是否能记住 `anden` 的例子？

### 8.5 Swedish Long Vowels

##### 中文总结

长元音要按单独音质学习，尤其是圆唇音 `y`, `ö`, `u`。`i/y` 和 `e/ö` 的差别主要在圆唇。

##### 检查点

- 是否能列出瑞典语 9 个长元音？
- 是否能通过圆唇练习 `i -> y`, `e -> ö`？
- 是否知道 `u` 是较难音？

### 8.6 Swedish Short Vowels

##### 中文总结

短元音不只是长元音缩短。短 `u` 特别重要；短 `e` 和短 `ä` 在多数地区相同。非重读元音也不弱化成统一的 e。

##### 检查点

- 是否能听辨 `bus/buss`？
- 是否知道 short `e` 常等于 short `ä`？
- 是否知道瑞典语非重读元音不简单弱化？

### 8.7 Pronunciation Of Ö And Ä Before R

##### 中文总结

`ö` 和 `ä` 在 `r` 前会有特殊音值。初学时重点是听辨，自己发成普通 `ö/ä` 通常不严重。

##### 检查点

- 是否能比较 `häl/här`？
- 是否知道 `e` 也可能表示这个短 `ä` 音？
- 是否理解地区差异？

### 8.8 Pronunciation Of The Letter O

##### 中文总结

字母 `o` 可能读作 `o`，也可能读作 `å`，尤其短 `å` 常写作 `o`。字母 `å` 本身不会造成问题，总读 `å` 音。

##### 检查点

- 是否知道 `o` 不是总读 o？
- 是否能记住 `jobb` 是短 `å` 音？
- 是否能区分 `kort` 的两个读音/意义？

### 8.9 Swedish Consonants

##### 中文总结

辅音可按发音部位、发音方式和清浊分类。本章重点看 stops 和 fricatives。

##### 检查点

- 是否能说明 consonant 的气流受阻特点？
- 是否知道后面重点是 stops/fricatives？
- 是否能解释 voiced/voiceless？

### 8.9.1 Swedish Stops

##### 中文总结

Stops 是瞬间完全阻断气流的辅音。瑞典语有 `p/t/k` 和对应浊音 `b/d/g`。

##### 检查点

- 是否能列出三组 stop 对应？
- 是否能说明 voiceless/voiced pairing？
- 是否注意 Swedish `t/d` 的舌位？

### 8.9.2 Swedish Fricatives

##### 中文总结

瑞典语 fricatives 中 `sj` 和 `tj` 最需要单独练。瑞典语没有英语 z 和 th 的对应音。

##### 检查点

- 是否能区分 `sj` 和 English sh？
- 是否知道 `tj` 可由 `k/kj` 拼写？
- 是否知道 Swedish lacks English `th`？

### 8.10 Other Consonants

##### 中文总结

`j` 类似英语 yes 的 y；`ng` 像 singer，不像 finger；瑞典语 `l` 总是清晰 l；词尾 `r` 不脱落。

##### 检查点

- 是否能正确读 `j`？
- 是否知道 `ng` 的读法？
- 是否知道 Swedish final `r` is pronounced？

### 8.11 Consonant Combinations

##### 中文总结

瑞典语可有多个辅音连在元音前。词首 `kn`, `gn` 中的 `k/g` 要发音；元音后的 `gn` 读作 `ng+n`。

##### 检查点

- 是否知道 `knä` 中 k 发音？
- 是否知道 `vagn/lugn` 的 `gn` 读法？
- 是否知道三辅音词首通常以 s 开头？

### 8.12 Letters Combined With J

##### 中文总结

很多含 `j` 的拼写组合读作一个音，如 `sj`, `tj`, `hj`, `lj` 等。外来词中 `sch/sh/ch` 也可能表示 `sj` 或 `tj`。

##### 检查点

- 是否能认出 `sjuk`, `skjorta`, `stjärna` 的共同读音？
- 是否知道 `hjälpa/ljus` 中首辅音不单独发？
- 是否知道 `-tion/-sion` 常读作 `-sjon`？

### 8.13 G K And Sk Before Front Vowels

##### 中文总结

`g/k/sk` 在前元音前读音改变：`g -> j`, `k -> tj`, `sk -> sj`。前元音包括 `i, e, ö, ä, y`。

##### 检查点

- 是否能列出 front vowels？
- 是否知道 `k` before front vowel 读 `tj`？
- 是否能读 `skina/skön`？

### 8.14 Rt Rd Rn And Rs

##### 中文总结

瑞典中北部常把 `r+t/d/n/s` 合读成一个音。初学时重点是听辨；自己分开发通常不会导致误解。

##### 检查点

- 是否知道 `rt/rd/rn/rs` 可能合音？
- 是否能听辨 `fat/fart`？
- 是否知道跨词边界也可能发生？

### 8.15 Doubled Consonants

##### 中文总结

双写辅音表示长辅音，也提示前面的重读元音通常是短元音。`k` 不写作 `kk`，而用 `ck`。

##### 检查点

- 是否能解释 `hat/hatt`？
- 是否知道 `ck` 代替 `kk`？
- 是否能从拼写推断单元音词的元音长短？

### 8.16 Doubling Of M And N

##### 中文总结

`m` 只在两个元音之间双写；词尾即使长也通常只写一个 `m`。一些常见词词尾 `n` 不双写，且 `n` 在 `d/t` 前不双写。

##### 检查点

- 是否能解释 `komma` 和 `Kom!`？
- 是否知道 `kan -> kunna`？
- 是否知道 `n` before `d/t` 不双写？

### 8.17 Capital And Small Letters

##### 中文总结

瑞典语大小写比英语少用大写。语言、国籍、星期、月份、季节通常小写；书名电影名等通常只首词大写。

##### 检查点

- 是否知道 `engelska`, `svensk` 小写？
- 是否知道 `fredag`, `december` 小写？
- 是否能按瑞典语规则写作品标题？
